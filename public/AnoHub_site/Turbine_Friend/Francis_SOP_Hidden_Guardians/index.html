SOP: Hidden Guardians - Critical Overlooked Components

Critical SOP: Hidden Guardians

The Often-Ignored Components That Prevent Catastrophic Failure

⚠️ THE SILENT PROTECTORS

Context: While operators focus on visible components (bearings, coupling, guide vanes), several "hidden" components work silently to prevent catastrophic failure. These are rarely inspected until failure occurs.

  • Why "Hidden": Located inside sealed assemblies, underwater, or in rarely-accessed areas.
  • The Risk: Failure of these components can cause sudden, severe damage that takes months to repair.
  • Inspection Frequency: Often skipped during routine maintenance due to access difficulty.
SOP 1: Nose Cone Integrity

The runner nose cone reduces turbulence at the draft tube exit. If bolts loosen, the cone shifts, creating massive vibration and hydraulic imbalance.

1.1 The Failure Mechanism

Physics: Water exits the runner at high velocity (~15-20 m/s) and must smoothly transition into the draft tube. The nose cone shapes this flow.

If Loose: Cone wobbles during rotation, creating asymmetric flow → vibration → bolt fatigue → cone detachment → catastrophic runner imbalance.

1.2 Inspection Requirements

Access: Unit must be shut down, draft tube dewatered, access via tailrace hatch.

  1. Visual Check: Inspect nose cone for cracks or deformation (especially around bolt holes).
  2. Bolt Torque Verification: Using torque wrench, verify each bolt maintains specified torque.
  3. Loctite Presence: Check for blue residue indicating Loctite was properly applied.
NOSE CONE BOLT SPECIFICATIONS:
Bolt Size: M16 or M20 (varies by manufacturer)
Torque: 180-220 Nm (check manufacturer manual)
Thread Locker: Loctite 243 (blue, medium strength)
Inspection Frequency: Every major overhaul (3-5 years)

1.3 Re-torquing Procedure

  1. Clean bolt threads with wire brush
  2. Apply fresh Loctite 243
  3. Torque in star pattern (like wheel lugs) to prevent cone warping
  4. Wait 24 hours for Loctite to cure before refilling draft tube
SOP 2: Vacuum Breaker (Snifter) Valve

The vacuum breaker valve is mounted on the turbine head cover. During load rejection, it must open rapidly to admit air and prevent destructive vacuum collapse.

2.1 The Critical Function

Load Rejection Scenario: When grid trips, guide vanes slam closed in 3-5 seconds. Water column decelerates, creating vacuum (negative pressure) in the spiral casing.

Without Vacuum Breaker: Vacuum can reach -0.8 to -0.9 bar, creating massive suction force that:

  • Implodes head cover (catastrophic structural failure)
  • Damages spiral casing liner
  • Causes violent water hammer on restart

2.2 Test Procedure

Functional Test (Annual):

  1. Locate Valve: Typically 6-12" diameter valve on top of head cover with counterweight or spring mechanism.
  2. Manual Actuation: Lift disc by hand (unit stopped). Should move freely with minimal force.
  3. Seating Test: Release disc - should drop and seat with audible "clunk".
  4. Seal Check: With unit filled but stopped, valve should be airtight (no hissing sound).

2.3 Common Failures

Symptom Cause Remedy
Valve stuck closed Corrosion on hinge pin Disassemble, wire-brush, lubricate with anti-seize
Slow opening Weak counterweight spring Replace spring or add weight to counterweight arm
Air leakage during operation Worn rubber seat Replace rubber gasket (standard NBR material)
CRITICAL: Vacuum breaker MUST actuate within 2-3 seconds of pressure drop. Test during scheduled shutdown by partially closing guide vanes and monitoring vacuum gauge.
SOP 3: Spiral Case Silt Drainage

Problem: Silt-laden water enters the spiral casing during operation. Heaviest particles settle at the bottom, accumulating over months.

3.1 The Accumulation Mechanism

Francis turbines have high-velocity flow (15-25 m/s) that keeps most silt suspended. However, at the spiral casing's lowest point (6 o'clock position), flow velocity drops slightly, allowing silt to settle.

Consequences of Buildup:

  • Reduced effective casing volume → efficiency loss (~1-2%)
  • Uneven flow distribution to guide vanes → vibration
  • Silt hardens into concrete-like deposits if left >6 months

3.2 Weekly Flushing Procedure

  1. Locate Drain Valve: Bottom of spiral casing, typically 3-4" ball valve or gate valve.
  2. Safety: Unit can remain running during flushing (low flow only, <30% load recommended).
  3. Open Drain: Crack valve open slowly. Initial discharge will be thick, muddy water.
  4. Flush Duration: Continue until water runs clear (typically 30-60 seconds).
  5. Close Valve: Ensure valve seats tightly (no dripping).
  6. Log Entry: Record silt volume estimate and water clarity in maintenance log.
MONSOON SEASON PROTOCOL: During Jun-Sep when river silt is highest (>3000 ppm), increase flushing frequency to twice weekly or daily if sediment load is critical.

3.3 Hardened Deposit Removal

If Valve Won't Drain (indicating blockage):

  1. Shut down unit and drain spiral casing
  2. Access spiral casing via inspection manway
  3. Manually remove hardened silt using shovel and wire brush
  4. Flush with high-pressure water before refilling
SOP 4: Runner Hub Pressure Relief Channels

The runner hub has small (5-10mm) pressure relief holes that balance pressure behind the runner. These are critical for thrust bearing protection (ref: Thrust Balance SOP).

4.1 Inspection During Overhaul

Access: Only accessible when runner is lifted out for overhaul.

  1. Locate Holes: Typically 4-8 radial holes drilled through runner hub from back side.
  2. Visual Inspection: Use flashlight and mirror to inspect each hole for silt blockage.
  3. Wire Probe Test: Insert 5mm diameter wire probe through full depth of each hole to verify clearance.
  4. Water Flush: Use garden hose to flush each hole from both directions.

4.2 Clearing Procedure

For Partially Blocked Holes:

  • Drill wire brush (5mm diameter) on cordless drill - carefully clean hole without damaging walls
  • High-pressure water flush (100-150 bar)
  • Verify clearance with gauge wire

For Fully Blocked Holes:

  • DO NOT attempt to re-drill - risk of off-center hole damaging runner balance
  • Use carbide-tipped punch to break through blockage
  • Flush extensively after clearing
WARNING: If >50% of pressure relief holes are blocked, thrust bearing load can triple (ref: Thrust Balance SOP). This is a critical hidden failure mode - do not skip this inspection during overhaul.
Summary Checklist
  • Every Major Overhaul (3-5 years): Nose cone bolt torque verification + Loctite re-application
  • Annually: Vacuum breaker functional test (manual actuation + seal check)
  • Weekly: Spiral case silt drainage (30-60 second flush)
  • During Runner Removal: Hub pressure relief channel inspection and clearing
