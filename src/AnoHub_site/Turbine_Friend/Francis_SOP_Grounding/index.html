SOP: Shaft Grounding & Electrolysis

Critical SOP: Shaft Grounding & Electrolysis Protection

The "Silent Bearing Killer" - Preventing Electrical Pitting

⚠️ THE INVISIBLE DESTROYER

Context: Magnetic imbalances in the generator induce electrical voltage on the rotating shaft. Without proper grounding, this voltage arcs through the bearing oil film, creating thousands of microscopic craters (pitting) in the Babbitt metal.

  • Why It's Silent: Unlike mechanical wear that produces noise or heat, electrical pitting is nearly undetectable until the bearing suddenly fails catastrophically.
  • The Mechanism: Each voltage arc is tiny (~millivolts), but occurs thousands of times per second, vaporizing microscopic amounts of bearing material.
  • Economic Impact: A bearing that should last 10 years can fail in 18 months if shaft grounding is non-functional.
SOP 1: The Phenomenon - Shaft Voltage Generation

Understanding the root cause prevents treating symptoms instead of the disease.

1.1 Generator Magnetic Asymmetry

In a perfect generator, magnetic flux is perfectly balanced. In reality, manufacturing tolerances, rotor eccentricity, and winding imperfections create flux asymmetry.

Result: A small AC voltage (0.1-5V) is induced on the shaft, which rotates at generator speed (typically 600-750 RPM for vertical Francis units).

1.2 The Arc Path

Without grounding, the shaft voltage seeks the easiest path to ground. This path is through the bearing oil film:

  1. Oil Film as Insulator: Thin oil film (~10-50 microns) acts as a weak dielectric.
  2. Voltage Breakdown: When shaft voltage exceeds oil film breakdown voltage (~0.5-1.0V), an arc jumps from shaft → bearing → ground.
  3. Pitting Mechanism: Each arc vaporizes a microscopic amount of Babbitt metal, creating a "crater" ~1-10 microns deep.
  4. Cumulative Damage: Millions of arcs over months/years erode bearing surface, destroying oil film capability.
VISUAL SIGNATURE OF ELECTRICAL PITTING:
• Surface appears "frosted" or "etched" (vs. smooth mechanical wear)
• Random crater distribution (vs. directional scoring from debris)
• Often concentrated on load-bearing pads
• Microscope reveals sharp-edged craters (vs. smooth gouges)
SOP 2: The Grounding Brush Inspection

Purpose: The shaft grounding brush provides a low-resistance path to ground, diverting shaft voltage AWAY from bearings.

2.1 Location & Design

  • Typical Location: On turbine shaft between thrust bearing and generator coupling (accessible without major disassembly).
  • Design: Carbon or copper-graphite brush (similar to generator brushes) mounted in spring-loaded holder, pressing against machined slip surface on shaft.
  • Required Contact: Brush must maintain constant electrical contact even during shaft vibration.

2.2 Brush Wear Inspection

  1. Access: Locate brush housing (typically has inspection cover removable without unit shutdown).
  2. Visual Check: Inspect brush length:
    • New Brush: 40-50mm length (varies by manufacturer)
    • Service Limit: 15-20mm remaining (below this, spring tension inadequate)
    • Critical: <10mm remaining → Replace immediately
  3. Surface Condition:
    • Healthy: Smooth, even wear pattern across brush face
    • Problem: Uneven wear (brush cocked in holder) or cracking (poor material quality)
  4. Spring Tension: Verify spring is not compressed to coil-bind. Should provide 2-5 kg force on brush.
SOP 3: Cleaning the Slip Surface

Critical: Even a perfect brush is useless if the shaft slip surface has high resistance due to contamination.

3.1 Contamination Sources

  • Silt Buildup: Airborne silt from powerhouse environment deposits on shaft (especially problematic for silt-heavy river units).
  • Oil Film: Bearing oil mist settles on shaft, creating insulating layer.
  • Oxidation: Shaft surface oxidizes if unit sits idle for extended periods (like Unit 1's shutdown).

3.2 Cleaning Procedure

  1. Safety: Unit must be fully stopped and locked out.
  2. Brush Removal: Retract or remove grounding brush to access slip surface.
  3. Initial Cleaning: Wipe slip surface with lint-free cloth dampened with electrical contact cleaner or isopropyl alcohol.
  4. Polishing: Use fine Scotch-Brite pad (equivalent to 600-grit) to gently polish surface, removing oxidation.
  5. Final Wipe: Clean again with alcohol-dampened cloth to remove polishing residue.
  6. Inspection: Surface should be bright metal, no discoloration or pitting visible.
  7. Reinstall Brush: Ensure brush seats properly on cleaned surface.
POST-CLEANING VERIFICATION:
Measure contact resistance (brush-to-shaft): Should be <0.1 Ω
If >0.5 Ω, cleaning insufficient or brush worn.
SOP 4: Voltage Measurement Protocol

Quantitative measurement provides objective proof of grounding effectiveness.

4.1 Equipment Required

  • True RMS Multimeter: Must measure AC+DC voltage accurately (cheap meters give false readings).
  • Test Leads: High-quality with minimal contact resistance.
  • Grounding Point: Verified earth ground (building steel, not painted surface).

4.2 Measurement Procedure

  1. Unit Running: Measurement must be taken during normal operation (shaft voltage only exists when generator is energized).
  2. Meter Setup: Set to AC voltage mode, auto-ranging or 5V scale.
  3. Probe Placement:
    • Negative (Black): Attach to verified ground point
    • Positive (Red): Touch to exposed shaft surface (NOT the grounding brush - measure bypass current)
  4. Reading Interpretation:
    SHAFT-TO-GROUND VOLTAGE LIMITS:
    Excellent: <0.2V (grounding highly effective)
    Acceptable: 0.2-0.5V (grounding functional)
    Warning: 0.5-1.0V (grounding marginal - inspect brush)
    Critical: >1.0V (grounding ineffective - bearing damage occurring)

4.3 Troubleshooting High Shaft Voltage

If measured voltage >0.5V:

  1. Inspect Brush Contact: Remove brush, check for excessive wear or contamination.
  2. Clean Slip Surface: Follow SOP 3 cleaning procedure.
  3. Check Brush Wiring: Verify grounding wire from brush holder to earth ground is intact (not corroded or broken).
  4. Measure Contact Resistance: Use ohmmeter to measure brush-to-shaft resistance. Should be <0.1Ω.< /li>
  5. Re-test: After corrective actions, re-measure shaft voltage. If still high, generator rotor may need re-balancing (specialist work).
Summary Checklist

Preventive Maintenance Schedule

  • Monthly: Visual inspection of grounding brush wear (through inspection window if available).
  • Quarterly: Measure shaft-to-ground voltage during operation. Record in logbook.
  • Annually: Full grounding system inspection:
    • Remove and inspect brush for wear
    • Clean slip surface
    • Measure contact resistance
    • Verify grounding wire integrity
  • After Extended Shutdown (>1 month): Clean slip surface before restart (oxidation accumulates during idle periods).

Bearing Pitting Detection

  • During Oil Analysis: High copper content in oil sample indicates Babbitt erosion → Check grounding.
  • During Bearing Inspection: "Frosted" or "etched" bearing surface → Likely electrical pitting → Verify grounding before reassembly.
  • Unexplained Bearing Failures: If bearing fails prematurely (<5 years) with no obvious mechanical cause → Investigate shaft grounding history.
