SOP: Thrust Bearing & Hydraulic Balance

Advanced SOP: Thrust Bearing & Hydraulic Balance

The "Forgotten" Balance - Axial Thrust Management & Balance Holes

⚠️ THE SILENT KILLER

Context: While the main bearings carry the turbine's weight (radial load), the thrust bearing handles the massive axial (horizontal) force created by water pressure pushing on the back of the runner.

  • The Risk: If the "balance holes" in the runner hub become clogged with silt, water pressure behind the runner increases dramatically, potentially tripling the thrust bearing load.
  • Why It's Forgotten: Thrust bearing temperature rise is gradual, so operators don't notice until Babbitt metal has already started to fail.
  • Silt-Heavy River Context: Unit 1's river carries significant silt, especially during monsoon season. Balance holes are highly susceptible to blockage.
SOP 1: Hydraulic Thrust Logic

Understanding the forces allows predictive detection, not just reactive repairs.

1.1 The Physics of Axial Thrust

Water enters the turbine at high pressure and exits at low pressure (draft tube). This pressure differential acts on the runner surfaces, creating a net force pushing the runner downstream (toward the generator).

SIMPLIFIED FORCE CALCULATION:
Effective Runner Area: ~1.5 m² (back side)
Upstream Pressure: 15 bar (150 m head)
Downstream Pressure: 0.2 bar (draft tube)

Net Thrust = (15 - 0.2) bar × 1.5 m² = ~220 kN (22 metric tons of force!)

Note: This is a massive force that continuously pushes on the thrust bearing pads.

1.2 The Balance Hole System

Design Intent: Balance holes (typically 4-8 holes drilled through the runner hub) allow high-pressure water to equalize on both sides of the runner, reducing net axial thrust.

When Functioning Properly: Balance holes reduce thrust by 50-70%, bringing the load from 220 kN down to ~80-100 kN.

When Clogged: No pressure equalization occurs. Full 220 kN load applied to thrust bearing → rapid temperature rise → Babbitt metal failure within hours.

SOP 2: Balance Hole Inspection Procedure

Criticality: Due to silt-heavy river conditions, balance hole inspection must be performed during every major overhaul and suspected whenever thrust bearing temperature increases without explanation.

2.1 Access Requirements

  • Unit Status: Complete shutdown, MIV closed, draft tube dewatered.
  • Safety: Confined space entry protocol for draft tube access (gas monitor, ventilation, safety harness).
  • Tools: High-pressure water lance, borescope camera, wire brushes, flashlight.

2.2 Visual Inspection

  1. Access via Draft Tube: Enter draft tube from tailrace side. Navigate to runner hub area.
  2. Locate Balance Holes: Typically 6-12mm diameter holes drilled radially through runner hub (usually 4-8 holes spaced evenly around circumference).
  3. Visual Check: With flashlight, inspect each hole opening:
    • PASS: Hole is clear, you can see through to the other side or confirm depth with wire probe.
    • FAIL: Hole is partially or fully blocked with silt, debris, or mineral deposits.

2.3 Cleaning Procedure

  1. Mechanical Cleaning: Use wire brush or flexible shaft drill to dislodge silt deposits.
  2. High-Pressure Water: Flush each hole with water lance (100-150 bar) to clear remaining debris.
  3. Verification: Insert wire gauge (6mm diameter) through full length of hole to confirm complete clearance.
  4. Documentation: Record condition of each hole in maintenance log (% blockage estimate before cleaning).
SILT-HEAVY RIVER PROTOCOL: During monsoon season (June-September), silt concentration triples. If unit operates continuously during this period, plan for balance hole inspection at end of season (October) as preventive maintenance, not just reactive.
SOP 3: Thrust Pad Inspection

The thrust bearing uses Babbitt metal pads that ride on an oil film. If the thrust load exceeds design limits (due to clogged balance holes), the oil film breaks down and metal-to-metal contact occurs.

3.1 Oil Analysis - The "Flakes" Test

Diagnostic Principle: Failing Babbitt metal sheds microscopic metallic flakes into the bearing oil. These flakes are visible proof of bearing distress.

  1. Sample Collection: Draw oil sample from thrust bearing sump (50ml minimum in clean glass bottle).
  2. Visual Inspection: Hold bottle to light source. Swirl gently.
    • PASS (Healthy): Oil is translucent amber, no particulates visible.
    • WARNING: Fine metallic "shimmer" visible when swirled - early stage wear.
    • CRITICAL: Visible metallic flakes settling to bottom - active Babbitt degradation.
  3. Laboratory Analysis: If flakes detected, send sample for spectrographic analysis to quantify metal concentration (ppm).

3.2 Thrust Pad Visual Inspection (During Overhaul)

Access: Requires disassembly of thrust bearing housing (major work, typically during scheduled outage).

  • Healthy Babbitt: Smooth silver surface, uniform thickness, no scoring or pitting.
  • Early Wear: Surface discoloration (darkening), minor scoring lines parallel to rotation.
  • Advanced Failure: Deep gouges, missing Babbitt chunks, exposed steel backing.

Recommended: Create visual reference chart posted in workshop for technician training.
SOP 4: Thrust Bearing Temperature Monitoring

Continuous monitoring provides early warning before catastrophic failure.

4.1 Normal Operating Range

THRUST BEARING TEMPERATURE LIMITS:
Normal: 40-50°C (healthy bearing, clean balance holes)
Warning: 50-60°C (increased load or marginal lubrication)
Critical: >60°C (STOP UNIT - inspect balance holes and oil quality)
Emergency: >70°C (Immediate shutdown - bearing failure imminent)

4.2 Diagnostic Correlation

Temperature rise patterns can indicate root cause:

  • Gradual rise over days/weeks: Balance holes slowly clogging with silt → Plan cleaning during next scheduled outage.
  • Sudden spike (hours): Balance hole(s) suddenly blocked by debris strike → Emergency inspection required.
  • Temperature rise with vibration increase: Possible misalignment creating unbalanced thrust → Check shaft alignment (ref: Precision SOP).

4.3 SCADA Integration

  • Alarm Setpoints: Configure SCADA to alarm at 55°C (warning) and 65°C (critical).
  • Trend Monitoring: Review thrust bearing temperature trend weekly. Gradual upward trend over months indicates balance hole degradation even if absolute temperature still acceptable.
Summary Checklist

Preventive Maintenance Schedule

  • Monthly: Collect oil sample from thrust bearing, visual flake inspection.
  • Quarterly: Review thrust bearing temperature trends (SCADA data).
  • Annually: Balance hole inspection and cleaning (mandatory for silt-heavy river conditions).
  • Every Major Overhaul (3-5 years): Disassemble thrust bearing, inspect Babbitt pads for wear, replace if >30% degraded.

Emergency Response Protocol

  • If thrust temperature >65°C: Reduce load immediately, prepare for shutdown.
  • If metallic flakes detected in oil: Plan emergency outage within 2 weeks maximum.
  • If temperature >70°C: STOP UNIT - Do not restart until root cause identified and corrected.
