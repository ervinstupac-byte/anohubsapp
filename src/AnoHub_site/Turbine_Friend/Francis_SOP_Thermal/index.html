The Infrared Eyes of Predictive Maintenance

Using infrared signatures to detect friction and electrical resistance before failure

‚ö†Ô∏è IR THERMOGRAPHY SAFETY

STOP. READ THIS FIRST.

  • Electrical Safety: Never point IR camera at high-voltage equipment while energized without proper PPE and training.
  • Arc Flash Risk: Maintain safe distances from exposed electrical connections (NFPA 70E guidelines).
  • Reflective Surfaces: Be aware that shiny metal surfaces reflect IR radiation from other hot sources, giving false readings.
The Thermal Grid: Critical Inspection Points

For horizontal Francis turbines, four primary thermal inspection points provide comprehensive health monitoring.

POINT A

Bearings (Turbine & Generator)

Target Temperature: < 60¬∞C (< 140¬∞F)

20¬∞C (Normal) 40¬∞C (OK) 60¬∞C (Limit) 80¬∞C (Critical)

What to Look For:

  • Uniform Temperature: Bearing housing should have even temperature distribution around circumference.
  • Hot Spots: Localized high temperature indicates misalignment, insufficient lubrication, or bearing damage.
  • Temperature Gradient: Sharp temperature rise from ambient to bearing = poor heat dissipation (cooling water issue).

Abnormal Thermal Signatures:

  • > 65¬∞C: Misalignment or vibration-induced friction. Check shaft run-out ‚Üí Alignment SOP
  • Asymmetric Heat: One side hotter = tilted bearing or uneven load. Inspect bearing clearances.
  • > 70¬∞C: Oil aeration or degraded lubricant. Perform oil analysis ‚Üí Oil Hygiene SOP
  • > 80¬∞C: CRITICAL - Stop unit immediately. Bearing failure imminent ‚Üí Bearings SOP
POINT B

Generator Stator Housing

Target Temperature: < 85¬∞C (< 185¬∞F) - varies by insulation class

What to Look For:

  • Uniform Heat Distribution: Stator should have relatively even temperature across its length.
  • Hot Spots: Localized high temperatures indicate insulation breakdown, shorted turns, or blocked cooling ducts.
  • Cooling Efficiency: Temperature should drop near cooling fan locations if air-cooled.

Abnormal Thermal Signatures:

  • Localized Hot Spot (> 95¬∞C): Insulation breakdown or shorted winding. Perform insulation resistance test ‚Üí Electrical Health SOP
  • Blocked Ventilation Pattern: High temperature in specific section = blocked cooling duct. Inspect and clean ventilation system.
  • Asymmetric Heating: Three-phase imbalance. Check phase currents and voltages.
  • > 100¬∞C (for Class F insulation): CRITICAL - Reduce load or stop unit to prevent insulation damage.
üí° Insulation Class Reference:
  • Class B: 130¬∞C maximum
  • Class F: 155¬∞C maximum
  • Class H: 180¬∞C maximum

Note: These are insulation ratings. Practical operating limits are 15-20¬∞C lower to ensure lifespan.

POINT C

Electrical Terminal Box

Target Temperature: < 50¬∞C above ambient (typically < 70¬∞C total)

What to Look For:

  • Connection Points: Scan all cable lugs, bus bars, and terminal connections.
  • Current Balance: All three phases should have similar temperatures (within ¬±5¬∞C).
  • Oxidation Detection: High resistance at connections due to oxidation causes heating.

Abnormal Thermal Signatures:

  • Hot Lug (> 80¬∞C): Loose bolt or oxidized connection. The "vibration effect" - vibration loosens bolts over time.
    Action: De-energize, re-torque all connections, clean oxidation with contact cleaner.
  • Phase Imbalance (> 10¬∞C difference): Unequal current distribution or loose connection on hot phase.
  • > 100¬∞C at any connection: CRITICAL - Immediate risk of arcing or fire. Shut down and repair.
  • Heat Pattern on Cable: If heat extends along cable from termination, cable itself may be undersized or damaged.
üí° The Vibration Effect:

Continuous generator vibration (even within acceptable limits) gradually loosens electrical connections. Thermal imaging detects this before catastrophic failure. Re-torque all terminal box connections annually.

POINT D

HPU Solenoids & Pumps

Target Temperature: Solenoids < 55¬∞C, Pumps < 65¬∞C

What to Look For:

  • Solenoid Coils: Should be warm (40-50¬∞C) when energized, cool when de-energized.
  • Pump Casing: Gradual temperature rise from inlet to outlet is normal (energy conversion).
  • Valve Bodies: Internal leakage ("stiction") manifests as localized heating.

Abnormal Thermal Signatures:

  • Solenoid > 70¬∞C: Internal leakage in valve causing continuous current draw. Replace valve ‚Üí HPU SOP
  • Pump Casing > 75¬∞C: Pump wear, cavitation, or bearing damage. Check pump performance curve.
  • Hot Spot on Valve Body: Internal "stiction" - spool valve stuck or binding. Indicates contamination in hydraulic oil ‚Üí Oil Hygiene SOP
  • Asymmetric Heating on Manifold: Flow imbalance or blockage in manifold passages.
IR Thermography Standards

Emissivity (Œµ) Settings - Critical for Accuracy

What is Emissivity? The ratio of IR radiation emitted by a surface compared to a perfect black body (Œµ = 1.0). Different materials emit different amounts of IR at the same temperature.

Common Turbine Materials & Emissivity Values:

Material / Surface Emissivity (Œµ) Application
Polished Stainless Steel 0.15 - 0.25 Shaft, runner (bare metal)
Oxidized Steel 0.75 - 0.85 Bearing housing (natural patina)
Painted Surfaces (Matte) 0.90 - 0.95 Generator housing, HPU
Electrical Insulation 0.85 - 0.90 Cable, terminal box
Copper (Oxidized) 0.60 - 0.70 Bus bars, electrical connections
Aluminum (Polished) 0.05 - 0.10 Heat sinks, reflectors

How to Set Emissivity on IR Camera

  1. Identify Surface Material: Know what you're measuring (painted metal vs. bare shaft).
  2. Consult Table Above: Select appropriate emissivity value.
  3. Camera Settings: Access emissivity menu on IR camera (usually "Œµ" or "Emis" setting).
  4. Verify with Contact Thermometer: For critical measurements, verify IR reading against a contact thermometer.
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Incorrect emissivity can cause 10-30¬∞C error in temperature reading. Always adjust for surface type. Shiny metal surfaces require lower Œµ settings!

Practical Tip: The Black Tape Method

For shiny surfaces where emissivity is unknown:

  1. Apply a small piece of electrical (black) tape to the surface.
  2. Wait 5 minutes for thermal equilibrium.
  3. Set camera emissivity to 0.95 (tape's emissivity).
  4. Measure tape temperature.
  5. Adjust emissivity until bare metal reads same as tape.
Baseline vs. Trend Analysis

Establishing Baseline Temperatures

When to Record Baseline:

  • During commissioning or after major overhaul
  • At normal operating load (typically 80-100% rated power)
  • After unit has reached thermal steady-state (minimum 2 hours of operation)
  • Under typical ambient conditions (note ambient temperature)

Baseline Recording Procedure

  1. Create Thermal Map: Sketch unit layout and mark all inspection points (A-D).
  2. Document Conditions: Record load (MW), ambient temp, cooling water temp, operating hours.
  3. Systematic Scan: Follow same path each time (clockwise from front).
  4. Capture Images: Save thermal images with clear labels (e.g., "Turbine_Bearing_DE_Baseline_2025-01-15").
  5. Log Data: Create spreadsheet with Point ID, Temperature, Load, Date.

Trend Analysis (6-Month Inspection)

How to Identify Abnormal Heat:

  • Compare to Baseline: Temperature increase > 10¬∞C from baseline = investigate.
  • Compare to Adjacent Similar Components: If one bearing is 15¬∞C hotter than identical bearing on other side = problem.
  • Track Over Time: Gradual temperature rise (5¬∞C per year) indicates degradation in progress.
  • Normalize for Ambient: Adjust for ambient temperature changes (rule of thumb: +1¬∞C component temp per +1¬∞C ambient).
üí° Example Trend Analysis:

Turbine Bearing (Point A):

  • Jan 2024 Baseline: 48¬∞C @ 5.0 MW, Ambient 22¬∞C
  • Jul 2024: 52¬∞C @ 5.0 MW, Ambient 28¬∞C ‚Üí (+4¬∞C component, +6¬∞C ambient) = Normal
  • Jan 2025: 62¬∞C @ 5.0 MW, Ambient 23¬∞C ‚Üí (+14¬∞C from baseline) = ABNORMAL - Inspect bearing
Environmental Bias - Seasonal Thermal Shifts

üåßÔ∏è Wet Season Thermal Adjustments

Context: High silt load (> 500 NTU) and cooler water temperatures affect thermal readings.

Thermal Behavior Changes:

  • cooling Efficiency Drops (Point A):
    Silt clogs bearing cooler tubes ‚Üí Heat transfer efficiency decreases.
    Symptom: Bearing temp rises despite cooler river water.
    Action: If bearing temp rises > 5¬∞C above baseline in wet season, check cooling water flow immediately.
  • False "Cool" Readings:
    Water splashing on turbine bearing housing (common in floods) cools surface skin.
    Risk: Internal bearing is hot, but housing looks cool on IR.
    Action: Measure temp at shielded location or oil outlet pipe.
  • Generator Heating (Point B):
    High humidity reduces air cooling efficiency.
    Action: Expect 3-5¬∞C higher stator interaction temps.

Inspection Schedule & Reporting
  • Monthly: Quick scan of all four points during routine rounds.
  • Bi-Annual: Full thermal survey with image capture and trend analysis.
  • After Load Changes: Scan after unit returns to service from extended outage.
  • Emergency: Immediate thermal scan if abnormal vibration, noise, or smell detected.
