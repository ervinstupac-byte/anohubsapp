Advanced SOP: Vortex Control & Draft Tube Dynamics

The "Forgotten" Fluid Dynamics - Managing Spiral Vortex & Surging

⚠️ THE SHAKING POWERHOUSE

Context: At specific load points (typically 40-60% power), a spiral vortex rope forms in the draft tube downstream of the runner. This is not a minor vibration - it can shake the entire powerhouse structure.

  • The Risk: Operators often dismiss this as "just noise" or "normal operation," but prolonged exposure causes:
    • Fatigue cracks in draft tube liner concrete
    • Bearing damage from cyclic loading
    • Floor bolt loosening in turbine mounting
  • Economic Impact: If ignored, vortex-induced fatigue can reduce turbine lifespan by 30-40%, requiring major refurbishment.
SOP 1: The Physics of Vortex Rope Formation

Understanding the mechanism allows intelligent countermeasures instead of reactive firefighting.

1.1 Fluid Dynamics Simplified

When the Francis runner operates at partial load, water exits the runner with residual swirl (tangential velocity). This swirl continues into the draft tube, creating a helical vortex rope that rotates and pulsates.

Key Physics: The vortex rope has a natural frequency (typically 0.2-0.4 × runner rotation frequency). If this frequency matches the structural resonance of the draft tube or powerhouse, violent vibrations occur.

1.2 Vortex Rope Characteristics

  • Appearance: If draft tube has sight glass, visible as a rotating "snake" of low-pressure vapor.
  • Frequency: Calculate expected vortex frequency:
    • Runner speed: 600 RPM (Unit 1) = 10 Hz
    • Vortex frequency: 0.2-0.4 × 10 Hz = 2-4 Hz
  • Audible Signal: Low-frequency "thumping" or "whumping" sound at 2-4 Hz interval (count: 1-Mississippi, 2-Mississippi rhythm).
  • Vibration Sensors: SCADA accelerometers will show peak at 2-4 Hz when vortex is active.
SOP 2: Identifying "Rough Running Zones"

Objective: Map the specific load points where vortex induces unacceptable vibration, then avoid operating in those zones.

2.1 Load-Shifting Diagnostic Procedure

  1. Test Setup: Install temporary vibration accelerometer on draft tube wall (or use existing SCADA sensors).
  2. Load Sweep: Incrementally adjust power output from 20% to 100% in 5% steps.
  3. Dwell Time: Hold each load for 3 minutes to allow vortex to stabilize.
  4. Data Recording: For each load point, record:
    • Vibration amplitude (mm/s RMS)
    • Dominant frequency (Hz)
    • Audible noise level (subjective: Low/Medium/High)
  5. Threshold Criteria:
    • Safe: <2.0 mm/s vibration
    • Warning: 2.0-4.0 mm/s (avoid continuous operation)
    • Critical: >4.0 mm/s (AVOID - damage accumulates rapidly)
TYPICAL ROUGH RUNNING ZONES FOR FRANCIS TURBINES:
• 35-50% load (low head conditions)
• 55-65% load (medium head, high swirl)

OPERATOR RULE: If grid demand requires operation in rough zone, limit continuous run time to <2 hours. Request load adjustment from dispatcher if possible.

2.2 Creating the "Forbidden Zone" Chart

After completing load sweep, create a visual chart posted in control room:

  • X-axis: Power output (0-100%)
  • Y-axis: Vibration level (mm/s)
  • Color zones: Green (safe), Yellow (warning), Red (avoid)

SOP 3: Air Injection Optimization

Air admission is not just "venting" - it's a precision tool to disrupt vortex coherence.

3.1 The Mechanism: Vortex Rope Breakup

Physics: Injecting air into the draft tube creates buoyant bubbles that interfere with the vortex rope's rotational coherence. The rope becomes unstable and breaks into smaller, less energetic eddies.

3.2 Tuning the Air Admission Valve

Context for Unit 1: Air admission valve is already installed (ref: DFT Air Admission SOP), but may not be optimized for vortex suppression.

  1. Baseline: Operate unit at known "rough running" load (e.g., 45% power) with air valve CLOSED. Record vibration level.
  2. Incremental Opening: Open air valve in 10% increments (10%, 20%, 30%... up to 100%).
  3. Observation: At each increment, allow 2 minutes for flow stabilization, then record vibration.
  4. Optimal Setting: The valve opening that produces minimum vibration is the "sweet spot" for this specific load point.
  5. Warning: Excessive air injection (>70% valve open) can create secondary noise and reduce efficiency slightly (~0.5-1%). Balance vibration reduction vs. efficiency loss.
Low-Head Operations: During monsoon season when river level is high (reducing net head), vortex intensity increases. Air injection requirements may double compared to dry season. Re-tune air valve seasonally.

3.3 Air Compressor Capacity Check

Ensure the air supply compressor can sustain the required flow rate:

  • Typical Demand: 50-100 Nm³/h at 2-3 bar gauge pressure
  • Verification: Monitor compressor pressure during peak air injection. If pressure drops below 1.5 bar, compressor is undersized.
  • Upgrade Path: If existing compressor insufficient, add secondary unit or increase tank capacity buffer.
SOP 4: Acoustic Diagnostic

Operators can identify vortex issues by sound even without instrumentation.

4.1 The "Thumping" Signature

  • Frequency: Low-frequency rhythmic thumping at 2-4 beats per second (120-240 BPM if thinking in music terms).
  • Location: Loudest near draft tube elbow or tailrace exit.
  • Character: "Whump-whump-whump" - not a high-pitched whine or grinding. Feels like a slow heartbeat.

4.2 Differential Diagnosis

Sound Likely Cause Action
Low-frequency thumping (2-4 Hz) Draft tube vortex rope Shift load or increase air injection
High-frequency whine (>100 Hz) Bearing issue or cavitation Refer to Bearings/Cavitation SOP
Grinding/scraping Mechanical contact (runner/casing) STOP UNIT - Inspect clearances
Intermittent banging Loose component or debris impact STOP UNIT - Inspect intake/runner
Summary Checklist
  • Quarterly: Perform load sweep to verify "rough running zones" chart is still accurate (river head changes seasonally).
  • Monthly: Test air admission valve for proper operation and optimal setting at typical loads.
  • Daily: During shift handover, report any new vibrations or unusual sounds to incoming operator.
  • On Overhaul: Inspect draft tube liner for fatigue cracks induced by long-term vortex exposure.
